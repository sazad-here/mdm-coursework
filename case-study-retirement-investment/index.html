1. Case Summary

This analysis examines the financial and demographic factors that influence how much an individual invests in tax-deferred retirement plans. Using dataset C09_04 with 194 observations, we investigate four independent variables: number of children in the household, annual salary (in thousands), mortgage balance (in thousands), and total other debt (in thousands). The dependent variable is the percentage of income invested in a tax-deferred retirement plan.

The dataset also includes a column labeled "Couple," which is a sequential observation index numbered 1 through 194. It is not a predictor variable and was excluded from all regression analyses. The four actual independent variables under investigation are Children, Salary (k), Mortgage (k), and Debt (k).

2. Regression Analysis

We began with an initial regression including all four independent variables. After examining p-values, Children (p = 0.954) was found to be statistically insignificant at α = 0.05 and was removed. A refined second regression was run using the three remaining significant predictors: Salary (k), Mortgage (k), and Debt (k).

Interpretation of Coefficients

All effects below are interpreted holding all other independent variables constant.

  • Salary (k): For every additional $1,000 in annual salary, the percentage of income invested increases by approximately 0.117 percentage points, holding Mortgage and Debt constant. Higher earners consistently allocate a greater share of income to retirement savings.
  • Mortgage (k): For every additional $1,000 in mortgage balance, the percentage invested decreases by approximately 0.054 percentage points, holding Salary and Debt constant. Larger housing obligations modestly suppress retirement contributions.
  • Debt (k): For every additional $1,000 in other debt, the percentage invested decreases by approximately 0.301 percentage points, holding Salary and Mortgage constant. With the largest per-unit effect, non-mortgage debt is the most significant financial barrier to retirement saving in this dataset.

3. Model Fit – R-Square and Standard Error

R-Square

The final model yields an R-Square of 0.4531 (45.3%), meaning the three predictors jointly explain approximately 45% of the variation in retirement investment rates. The Adjusted R-Square of 44.5% confirms this is a genuine fit, not inflated by the number of predictors. The F-test (F = 52.47, p = 9.45E−25) confirms the model is highly statistically significant. In behavioral financial research, explaining 45% of variance from three financial variables alone represents a strong and meaningful result.

Standard Error and the Empirical Rule

The Standard Error of the regression is 0.03366. Applying the empirical rule:

  • Approximately 68% of predictions fall within ±3.37 percentage points of the actual investment rate (±1 standard error).
  • Approximately 95% of predictions fall within ±6.73 percentage points of the actual rate (±2 standard errors).

For context: if the model predicts a person invests 8.0%, we can be 68% confident the true value falls between 4.6% and 11.4%, and 95% confident it falls between 1.3% and 14.7%.

6. Conclusion

Group Tendency

The data reveals a clear pattern: higher salary is positively associated with greater retirement investment, while both mortgage obligations and — most strongly — non-mortgage consumer debt suppress investment rates. Debt is the single most influential financial barrier to retirement saving in this sample. Number of children does not exert a statistically meaningful effect once financial variables are controlled.

Regression Success

The refined model is statistically successful. All three retained predictors are highly significant (p < 0.001), the model explains 45.3% of variation in retirement investment rates, and the F-statistic (52.47, p = 9.45E−25) confirms overall model robustness. Removing Children improved the Adjusted R-Square from 44.2% to 44.5%.

Missing Variables

Two additional variables could improve the model: Age (individuals closer to retirement typically contribute more) and Employer Matching Rate (a well-documented incentive for higher contribution rates). Both could be gathered through an employee benefits or HR survey.
